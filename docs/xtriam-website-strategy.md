## 2. The Dual Mission of the xTriam Website

The xTriam website currently serves two audiences and must serve both simultaneously — without cannibalizing the SEO performance built around bpmPro.

### Audience A — bpmPro prospects (primary, SEO-critical)
Window and door contractors, glazing companies, and home services businesses searching for CRM, BPM, or quoting software. These visitors arrive via organic search. xTriam currently ranks #1 for several target keywords in this vertical. **This ranking must be protected at all costs.**

### Audience B — AI agency prospects (emerging, brand-building)
Construction and trades business owners, operations managers, and potential collaborators who are evaluating xTriam as an AI automation partner — not just a software vendor. This audience is being introduced gradually, primarily through the About page and supporting content.

These two audiences are complementary, not conflicting. A contractor who finds xTriam via a bpmPro keyword search may, over time, become an AI automation client. The website should create that natural progression without confusing or diluting either message.

## 3. SEO Mandate — Protecting #1 Rankings

### Current position
xTriam holds #1 rankings for several high-intent keywords in the window and door CRM/BPM space. These rankings represent years of content investment and must be treated as a protected asset.

### What the agent must NOT do
- Introduce new primary keywords or topic clusters that compete with or dilute the existing bpmPro keyword strategy
- Restructure URL slugs, page titles, or H1s on pages that currently rank well without explicit approval
- Add AI agency language to pages that are currently performing for bpmPro-related terms
- Trigger Google re-indexing signals that could disrupt existing SERP positions
- Publish content that sends mixed intent signals on high-performing pages (e.g., adding "AI agency" copy to a page ranking for "window and door CRM")

### What the agent SHOULD do
- **Deepen page content** on bpmPro feature pages — add more specific, long-form content about BPM workflows, quoting processes, production scheduling, project accounting, and Salesforce integration for the window and door industry
- **Add supporting content** (blog posts, FAQs, use case pages) that expand the semantic footprint around existing ranked keywords without changing those pages directly
- **Strengthen internal linking** between bpmPro feature pages and supporting content
- **Improve E-E-A-T signals** — author credentials, industry-specific detail, real client outcomes, and specificity that generic competitors cannot match
- **Optimize for long-tail** — target supporting keywords that reinforce the core cluster (e.g., "Salesforce for glazing contractors," "quoting software for impact window companies," "BPM for home services")

## 4. Page Depth Strategy — bpmPro Content

Each bpmPro feature area should have substantial, specific content that demonstrates genuine industry knowledge. Generic software descriptions do not rank or convert in this vertical. Depth signals authority.

### Priority pages to deepen

**CPQ / Quoting**
Content should cover: how impact window and door quoting works in practice, county tax handling, product configurator logic, margin and markup mechanics, how quotes become contracts, mobile quoting in the field. Target buyer: estimators and sales reps at window companies.

**Production Scheduling**
Content should cover: how installation crews are assigned, job sequencing, permit tracking, material delivery coordination, field crew communication. Target buyer: operations managers and project coordinators.

**Project Accounting**
Content should cover: job costing methodology, GP tracking per project, budget vs. actual reporting, how payment milestones connect to project stages, QuickBooks sync. Target buyer: owners and controllers.

**Payments (Stripe)**
Content should cover: how contractors collect deposits and progress payments, ACH vs. card, payment link delivery via SMS/email, how payment status flows back into the job record. Target buyer: office managers and owners.

**Communications (Twilio / SendGrid)**
Content should cover: automated SMS and email triggers at each project stage, client-facing update cadence, how communication reduces inbound "where's my job" calls. Target buyer: customer service and office staff.

**Each feature page should include:**
- A plain-language explanation of the problem this feature solves (written for a contractor, not a developer)
- A step-by-step description of how the feature works in practice
- At least one real-world scenario or workflow example
- A short FAQ section addressing the most common questions from this role
- Internal links to related features and supporting content

## 5. AI Agency Introduction — Gradual, About Page First

### The strategic constraint
xTriam does not want to send Google signals that it is pivoting away from CRM/BPM software for window and door professionals. Any AI agency positioning must be introduced carefully, starting on pages that are not part of the current ranking strategy.

### The right entry point: the About page
The About page is the correct place to introduce the broader xTriam mission. It is not a transactional page (it does not rank for product-intent keywords), and it is the natural place for a company to explain who it is and where it is going.

### How to frame the AI agency mission on the About page

The framing should feel like a natural evolution, not a pivot. The narrative arc is:

> "We built bpmPro because we lived the operational chaos it solves. That experience — 17 years running a construction business, then building software for it — taught us something broader: the construction and trades industry is sitting on enormous savings potential that AI can unlock. bpmPro is our most mature product. But the mission is bigger: helping home services companies become more profitable by replacing manual workflows with AI agents."

**Language to use:**
- "Profitability through process automation" (xTriam's core tagline)
- "AI-powered operations for home services companies"
- "From field experience to intelligent automation"
- "We help contractors do more with the team they already have"

**Language to avoid on the website (for now):**
- "AI agency" — too generic, competes with unrelated search intent
- "Replace your employees with AI" — wrong tone for this audience
- Anything that implies xTriam is leaving the Salesforce/bpmPro space

### Phased introduction plan

**Phase 1 (now):** About page only. Update the company description to reflect the broader mission. No new pages, no new keyword targets. One clear paragraph about AI automation as xTriam's direction.

**Phase 2 (month 2–3):** Add a standalone "AI Automation" or "Services" page with its own URL — separate from all bpmPro pages. This page can describe the agency offering without touching existing ranked content.

**Phase 3 (month 4+):** Build out supporting content (case studies, ROI examples, blog posts) around the AI automation offering. At this stage, internal links can begin connecting bpmPro pages to the AI services page — framing it as a natural extension of the product.

## 9. How the Audience Thinks — Search Intent vs. Industry Terminology

This is one of the most important things the website agent must understand. The people xTriam wants to reach as an AI automation partner do not know what an "AI agency" is. They do not search for it. They search for the symptom — the daily frustration — not the solution category.

**The fundamental rule:** "AI agency" is an internal category label. It should almost never appear in public-facing copy. The website meets visitors at their pain, in their language, and walks them toward the solution.

### What they search vs. what we call ourselves

## 11. The Contractor Awareness Funnel — Where Visitors Are When They Arrive

Most visitors to the AI automation content will be at Stage 1 or 2. The content strategy must walk them down this path — not assume they are already ready to buy.

**Stage 1 — Problem unaware** ("this is just how business is")
They are frustrated but have not identified a fixable problem. They think slow quoting, missed follow-ups, and admin overload are just the cost of running a trades business. Content goal: name the pain and assign a dollar figure to it. Example headline: *"Your quoting process is costing you $60K/yr and you don't know it."*

**Stage 2 — Problem aware** ("I need to fix this somehow")
They know something is broken. They search "how to reduce back office costs" or "automate invoicing." They do not yet know AI is the tool. Content goal: show that automation is the answer, and it is simpler than they think.

**Stage 3 — Solution aware** ("I've heard about automation / AI for this")
They have seen something about AI tools or workflow automation. They are curious but skeptical — they don't know if it applies to their specific trade business. Content goal: make it concrete and industry-specific. Not generic "AI" — "AI that generates a window and door quote in 10 minutes."

**Stage 4 — Product aware** ("I want someone to do this for my business")
They are actively looking for a partner or tool. Now they may search "AI automation consultant trades" or "workflow automation for contractor business." Content goal: make xTriam the most credible, specific choice. ROI proof, case studies, the audit offer.

**Stage 5 — Most aware** ("I'm ready to talk to xTriam")
They have read the content, seen the results, or received a referral. They need only a frictionless next step. Content goal: clear, low-commitment CTA — the free AI Readiness call. No friction, no pressure.

## 12. Approved Language — Messaging Guidelines for All Content

### The one-sentence company description (use this everywhere)
> "We help construction and home services companies automate the manual work that's eating their margins — quoting, scheduling, invoicing, collections, customer updates — so they can grow without adding overhead."

No mention of AI. No mention of agency. Pure outcome. If a visitor asks how — then the technology is introduced.

### Approved taglines and phrases

| Phrase | Best used for |
|---|---|
| "Profitability through process automation" | Core tagline — use everywhere |
| "We help home services companies do more with the team they already have" | About page intro |
| "Stop paying people to do work a machine can handle in seconds" | Pain-stage blog content, LinkedIn |
| "Your estimator's time is worth more than building quotes by hand" | Quoting-focused content |
| "We automate the back office so your team can focus on the work that actually pays" | Services page description |
| "Built by someone who ran a construction company — not by someone who read about it" | Credibility sections, bios |
| "Your best people shouldn't be doing $15/hr work" | LinkedIn, ads, high-attention hooks |
| "Scale without adding headcount" | ROI-focused content, sales pages |
| "Run lean. Bill more. Keep your team." | Short-form copy, tagline variant |

### Tone rules for all content
- **Outcome first, technology second.** Lead with what the contractor gets. Introduce the method (AI, automation) only after the outcome is established.
- **Specific over vague.** "75% faster quoting" beats "significantly faster quoting." Real numbers always.
- **Operator to operator.** Write as someone who has been on a job site, not as a tech vendor.
- **No startup language.** Words like "leverage," "ecosystem," "scalable solutions," and "disruptive" are invisible to contractors. Plain language only.
- **Bilingual awareness.** A significant portion of the South Florida home services market is Spanish-speaking. Consider bilingual versions of key pages and CTAs selectively.

### Language the agent should never use in contractor-facing content
- "AI agency" — internal label only
- "AI agent" / "AI agents" — too technical, wrong connotation for this audience
- "Leverage AI" — generic, means nothing
- "Digital transformation" — enterprise jargon
- "Cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art" — overused, zero credibility
- "Disruptive" — startup language
- "Synergy" / "ecosystem" — wrong register entirely
- Any claim of being "the only" or "the best" without a specific, verifiable qualifier
